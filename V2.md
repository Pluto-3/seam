# seam v2 — sketch, not a plan yet

This is a carry-forward document, written at the end of the v1 session to hand a
fresh session a clear starting picture. Nothing here is built. Nothing here is
phased out yet. It's the shape of the conversation that led to it, condensed.

Read `DESIGN.md` and `LOG.md` first regardless — v2 doesn't discard v1, it builds
on proven logic. The mechanics (scarcity produces trade, the seven actions,
the congestion fix, the emergency-food-routing fix, the argmax-fallback pattern
for LLM agents) are settled and correct. What changes in v2 is depth, identity,
persistence, and the technology carrying all of it.

## Why v2, in the user's own words

"v2 is for personal fascination, but also something id show people, run
continuously, and eventually make money, utilize every dimension of it."

Not one of these traded off against the others — all four, simultaneously.
That's the actual design constraint: whatever gets built needs to hold up as a
personal project, a showable one, an always-on one, and a monetizable one, at
the same time.

"This aint no poc no more" — v1 proved the concept was sound. v2 is not
expected to be built with v1's prototyping-era shortcuts (Python chosen for
iteration speed, pygame as a quick local viewer, flat files for data). Tech
stack is explicitly open for reconsideration wherever it serves the goal
better.

## What "alive" actually means here

The reference point, in the user's words: Rick checking CCTVs on his computer
to see what's happening in the microverse, and climbing in himself when he
wants to. Not literal sci-fi — the functional pattern: **passive monitoring
from outside, and active immersive interaction on demand**, both pointed at a
world that keeps existing whether or not anyone's watching it.

Concretely, "alive" means:
- You can check in and actually understand what's going on, not just see a
  number change.
- The world teaches you something over time (this is where the exporters -
  strategy, data, narrative - are supposed to matter, and currently don't).
- You can go in and interact when you want to, not just watch.

## What v1 actually proved vs. what's missing

Proven, with real verification behind each claim (see `LOG.md`):
- Scarcity + specialization produces real trade (20-seed paired comparison).
- Goal-driven LLM agents behave differently without being scripted to (40
  verified decisions, zero fallbacks needed in the real test).
- A local LLM integration that never blocks the simulation, even when the
  model hangs.
- A population-collapse bug found, fixed, and confirmed at full scale.

Missing - and this is the actual substance of v2, not polish on top of v1:
- **Leads have no memory.** Every decision is a stateless prompt. No
  accumulated experience, no relationships, no arc across a run.
- **The narrative is templates, not writing.** "Tick 4200 - lead0 trades ore
  for wood with a12" is an audit log, not a story anyone would read.
- **Nobody can tell who's who.** A lead is a gold ring around a dot. No
  visible identity anywhere in the interface.
- **The world doesn't persist.** It starts fresh on launch and ends when the
  window closes - the opposite of "a world that keeps existing."
- **The exporters don't feed anything.** Three working exporters, zero real
  consumers - no Waypoint integration, no dataset anyone's used, no
  narrative published anywhere.
- **The player has no reason to exist.** Confirmed directly: "i never used
  any of them, never really knew what im posed to do with them." Move-and-
  press-a-key isn't a reason to engage - there's no goal, no stakes, nothing
  to protect or chase. This is a real open design question, not yet solved -
  see below.

## Locked decisions

**Crowd tier stays mechanical, cheap, and numerous.** This was discussed and
resolved directly: crowd agents do not get LLM reasoning. Every LLM call
costs real time even locally: if every agent reasoned, the population would
have to shrink to the size of today's lead cast, and the world would feel
empty rather than populated. The crowd's job is to *be the economy* - the
proven mechanic - not to be individually interesting. What crowd agents *do*
get in v2: a name and a one-line identity, generated once at spawn (a single
batched call for the whole crowd, not per-tick), so zooming in on any one of
them in the monitoring view reveals someone, not "a12" - cheap flavor, not
behavioral depth.

**Lead tier stays small, gets deep instead of wide.** Not scaling up the
*number* of LLM-driven agents - 2 to 4 is the right range. A real story needs
a small cast, not twenty simultaneous reasoning agents competing for the same
local model. What grows is depth per lead:
- **Memory** - accumulated experience feeding back into future decisions, so
  a lead's behavior can genuinely develop (a trader burned by a bad run of
  trades becomes more cautious; a protector who's watched a settlement
  starve gets more aggressive about it) rather than repeating the same
  goal-following pattern on tick 50 and tick 5000.
- **Visible identity** - name, goal, personality actually shown in the
  viewer, not just present in code.

**Real narrative generation, not templates.** Periodic LLM-authored prose
summarizing what actually happened - reusing the same async/never-blocks
pattern already proven necessary the hard way in v1 (see the "crashing like
hell" incident in `LOG.md`) - not a per-event flood, a scene/summary cadence.
This is where the actual spectacle payoff lives.

**Architecture: split the simulation from the viewer.** Currently they're the
same process - the world only exists while `watch.py` is open. v2 splits this
into a **persistent simulation service** (the world ticks continuously,
independent of anyone watching) and a **viewer** that connects, watches,
allows possession, and disconnects without stopping the world underneath.
This single change delivers the hybrid persistence requirement directly
("continuous persistence yes, but maybe hybrid... start watch close cycle not
bad cause i have other things to do sometimes") - leave the service running
for continuous mode, or just don't start it for the on-demand cycle - and it's
literally the CCTV pattern: the viewer is the monitor, the service is the
world existing independent of being watched.

**Tech stack.** Explicitly not staying Python-for-everything - v1's stack was
chosen for prototyping speed, and that tradeoff no longer applies.
- **Simulation core: Rust.** Performance and safety for something meant to
  run continuously and eventually scale up substantially; no GC-pause risk in
  the tick loop. This is a port of already-proven logic (world/agents/
  decide/actions/tick from the Python version, including the congestion fix
  and emergency-food-routing fix), not a redesign from scratch - lower risk
  than a rewrite usually implies.
- **LLM orchestration (leads' reasoning, narrative generation): Python**, as
  a sidecar service talking to the Rust core over an API. This part is
  I/O-bound, not compute-bound, and Python's ecosystem here is a genuine
  strength worth keeping.
- **Viewer: React (web), replacing pygame entirely.** Pygame is local-desktop
  only - no remote access, no multiple viewers, limited UI. A web dashboard
  over a WebSocket (live state feed) plus REST (control: possess, adjust
  settings, pull reports) delivers the actual CCTV/monitoring vision and is
  the only realistic path to "something I'd show people."
- **Data layer: PostgreSQL**, replacing flat CSV/JSONL files. If "data is
  money" is ever going to be real, the exports need to be queryable and
  servable, not files someone has to be handed.

**Data is money: plumbing stays live, monetization stays deferred.** Not
actively chasing revenue in v2. But every system - exporters, the data layer,
the narrative feed - should stay functional and evolve alongside richer
leads/crowd data, specifically so that when an opportunity arises, it's a
matter of turning something on, not rebuilding it. Explicitly not a v2
build target on its own.

## Open questions - genuinely unresolved, not just unbuilt

- **What is the player's actual purpose?** Confirmed as a real problem, not
  yet solved. Possession without a goal is a tech demo, not a reason to
  engage. Needs an actual answer before the hatch is worth building further -
  something to protect, pursue, compete over, or influence.
- **What does lead memory look like mechanically?** Raw event history?
  Periodic LLM-generated self-summaries fed back into future prompts?
  Something that can influence a lead's *goal*, not just its immediate
  decisions? Not designed yet.
- **What does narrative generation look like concretely?** Cadence (every N
  ticks? per notable event cluster?), format, and where it's actually
  surfaced (in the viewer live, a periodic digest, both) - not decided.
- **How far does "real ass world" scale go, and when?** Validated as a
  legitimate long-term direction, explicitly not a v2 requirement. The
  service/Rust architecture is meant to be a foundation that doesn't block
  this later, not something v2 needs to fully realize.
- **Build order / phasing** - not sketched yet. This document is the picture,
  not the plan. Next session's first job is turning this into staged,
  verifiable phases the way v1's Phase 0-3 were built.
